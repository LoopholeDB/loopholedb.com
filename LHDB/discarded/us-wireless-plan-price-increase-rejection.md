# Rejecting a Wireless Carrier's Unannounced Price Increase

## What Is It?

Wireless carriers — AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile, and others — regularly raise prices on existing plans, sometimes with little or no meaningful notice. What most customers don't know: if a carrier changes the material terms of your service agreement (including price) without adequate notice, you may be able to:

1. **Reject the increase and demand the original rate** (or a credit)
2. **Cancel your service without an early termination fee (ETF)**
3. **File an FCC complaint** that triggers a formal carrier response

## How It Works

### Step 1 — Check your original agreement and notice

Pull up the original service agreement or confirmation email from when you signed up or last changed plans. Carriers are bound by their own terms of service, which typically require advance notice (30 days is standard) before any material change.

If you received no notice — or notice came in a buried email the same month the charge appeared — document that.

### Step 2 — Call and dispute immediately

Call customer service and say clearly:

> *"I did not agree to a price increase on my plan. I'd like to dispute this change and request that my rate be restored, or receive a credit for the difference. If you can't do that, I'd like to know my options for cancelling without a penalty under your change-in-terms policy."*

Key phrases that prompt escalation:
- **"Material change in terms"**
- **"Change-in-terms cancellation right"**
- **"I did not accept this modification"**

Most front-line agents have authority to issue a one-time credit or escalate to retention. Retention agents have much more flexibility.

### Step 3 — Get it in writing

Ask for a case or reference number. Follow up with a written complaint via the carrier's online support chat or email so you have a paper trail.

### Step 4 — Escalate if needed

If the carrier refuses to restore your rate or waive the ETF:

- **File an FCC complaint** at [fcc.gov/consumers/guides/filing-informal-complaint](https://www.fcc.gov/consumers/guides/filing-informal-complaint). Carriers are required to respond to FCC complaints directly, usually within 30 days. This often unlocks account credits or restorations that front-line agents won't offer.
- **File a CFPB complaint** if the billing change involves financing or device payment plans.
- **Contact your state attorney general's consumer protection division** — many states have additional unfair business practice statutes.

## What Most People Don't Know

- **The "change-in-terms" exit right is real.** Most carrier service agreements include a clause that allows you to cancel without an ETF if the carrier makes a material change to your contract. A price increase almost always qualifies. Carriers count on customers not knowing this.
- **"Administrative fee" increases are still price increases.** Carriers often disguise rate hikes as new or increased "administrative fees" or "economic adjustment charges." These are still changes to your cost of service and are disputable on the same grounds.
- **Loyalty matters in negotiation.** Long-tenured customers consistently get better outcomes. Mention your tenure explicitly — carriers track churn risk.
- **FCC complaints work.** A formal FCC complaint routes to the carrier's executive relations team, not a call center. Response rates and resolution rates are significantly higher.

## Who Benefits Most?

Any customer on a month-to-month or postpaid plan who has seen their bill increase without agreeing to a new plan. Customers on "price lock" promotions (e.g., T-Mobile's Price Lock guarantee) have even stronger standing to dispute.

## Important Caveats

- **There is no federal law requiring carriers to give notice.** Unlike Canada's CRTC Wireless Code, the US has no equivalent regulation mandating advance notice of price increases or a universal right to cancel. Your rights come from the carrier's own service agreement — read it.
- **Most US carriers eliminated 2-year contracts around 2015–2016.** Modern plans are month-to-month for service, even if you have a device payment plan. This means "ETF" concerns mainly apply to device financing, not the service plan itself.
- **"Material adverse change" is vague.** Carriers define this in their own agreements and can argue small increases don't qualify. Act quickly — you typically have 14–60 days from notice to cancel under this provision.
- **Continuing to use service = acceptance.** If you keep using your phone after a price change, carriers treat that as acceptance of the new rate. You must affirmatively object and cancel within the notice window.

## Legal Basis

- **FCC Consumer Complaint Process** — The FCC has jurisdiction over interstate telecommunications services under the Communications Act of 1934 (47 U.S.C. § 151 et seq.).
- **FTC Act § 5** — Unfair or deceptive acts or practices in commerce, applicable to misleading billing and inadequate notice.
- **State Consumer Protection Laws** — Most states have UDAP (Unfair and Deceptive Acts and Practices) statutes that apply to utility and telecom billing.
- **Carrier's own Terms of Service** — The change-in-terms exit right is typically in the carrier's own published agreement, making it contractually enforceable.
