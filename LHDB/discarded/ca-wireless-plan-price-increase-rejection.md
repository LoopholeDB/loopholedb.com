## Overview

The **CRTC Wireless Code** (mandatory since 2013, updated 2017 and 2022) is a legally binding code of conduct for all wireless carriers in Canada. It gives you clear, enforceable rights when a carrier tries to change your plan terms — including price increases. Most Canadians never use these rights because carriers make the process deliberately opaque.

## Your Rights Under the CRTC Wireless Code

Your rights depend on whether you are on a **fixed-term contract** (e.g., a 24-month plan) or a **month-to-month** arrangement.

### Fixed-term contract customers (strongest protection)

| Right | Details |
|---|---|
| **Price cannot change mid-contract** | Price is a "key contract term" — the carrier is outright prohibited from raising it during your commitment period without your express consent |
| **Right to cancel without penalty** | If the carrier violates this and raises your price anyway, you can cancel with no early cancellation fee |
| **30-day notice for other changes** | Non-price changes (e.g., data policy, roaming terms) still require 30 days' advance written notice |

### Month-to-month customers (moderate protection)

| Right | Details |
|---|---|
| **30-day advance notice required** | The carrier must notify you at least 30 days before a price increase takes effect |
| **Right to leave** | If you don't accept the change, your remedy is to cancel — there is generally no ETF on month-to-month plans anyway |
| **Clarity on fees** | All fees must be clearly itemized — vague "administrative fees" not in your original terms are still disputable |

## How to Dispute a Price Increase

### Step 1 — Document the change

Check your original plan confirmation and compare it to your current bill. Note:
- The original price vs. the new price
- Whether you received a 30-day advance notice (email, text, or bill insert)
- The date the increase first appeared on your bill

### Step 2 — Call and dispute

Call your carrier's customer service and say clearly:

> *"Under the CRTC Wireless Code, I am entitled to 30 days' notice before any negative change to my plan. I [did not receive this notice / do not accept this change]. I'm requesting my original rate be restored or a credit issued. If you cannot do that, I'd like to cancel without penalty under the Wireless Code."*

Ask for a case reference number. Escalate to a manager or retention team if the first agent refuses.

### Step 3 — Follow up in writing

Submit a written complaint through the carrier's online portal or by email. This creates a paper trail and often prompts a faster resolution from a senior team.

### Step 4 — File a CCTS complaint

If the carrier does not resolve your complaint within 30 days, escalate to the **Commissioner for Complaints for Telecom-television Services (CCTS)**:

- Website: [ccts-cprst.ca](https://www.ccts-cprst.ca)
- The CCTS is free, independent, and has authority to order carriers to provide remedies
- Carriers are required to participate in the CCTS process
- Most complaints are resolved within 40 days

## What Most People Don't Know

- **"Administrative fees" are still price increases.** Carriers often disguise rate hikes as new "network access fees" or "system improvement charges." If it wasn't in your original agreement, it's a change to your terms — and you have the same rights.
- **Month-to-month customers also have rights.** Even if you're not on a fixed-term contract, the carrier must still give 30 days' notice and you can cancel without penalty if they raise your price without it.
- **The CCTS has real teeth.** A CCTS complaint routes to the carrier's regulatory affairs team. Resolution rates are high — carriers don't want CCTS escalation records.
- **MVNO customers are also covered.** If you're with Fido, Koodo, Virgin, Public Mobile, or any other reseller, the Wireless Code still applies.

## Provincial Overlay

Some provinces add protections on top of the federal Wireless Code:
- **Quebec**: Under the Consumer Protection Act, distance contracts (including wireless) have a 10-day cancellation right from signing, and any unilateral contract modification triggers a right to terminate.
- **Ontario / BC / Alberta**: Provincial consumer protection offices can receive complaints about deceptive billing practices independently of the CCTS.

## Legal Basis

- **CRTC Wireless Code** — Mandatory code under the *Telecommunications Act* (S.C. 1993, c. 38), enforced by the CRTC. Decision 2013-271, updated by Decision 2017-200 and Broadcasting and Telecom Decision 2022-179.
- **Commissioner for Complaints for Telecom-television Services (CCTS)** — Independent dispute resolution body mandated by the CRTC.
- **Quebec Consumer Protection Act** — R.S.Q. c. P-40.1, ss. 54.4–54.16 (distance contracts and unilateral amendment).
