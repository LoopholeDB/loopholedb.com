## Overview

When you buy a **new or substantially renovated home** in Canada, you pay GST (5%) or HST (up to 15%) on the purchase price — a significant cost on a $600,000 home. The **GST/HST New Housing Rebate** allows you to recover a portion of that tax: up to **$6,300 federally**, plus up to **$24,000 under provincial programs** (notably Ontario's), for a potential total rebate of **$30,000**.

The rebate is well-known in real estate circles but is often handled quietly by builders — many buyers don't realize how much they paid in HST or how the rebate works. In some cases, builders assign the rebate to themselves at closing as part of the deal; in others, buyers must file themselves. Understanding which situation applies to you can mean thousands of dollars.

## Federal GST/HST New Housing Rebate

**The basic calculation:**
- The federal rebate equals **36% of the 5% GST** paid on the purchase, up to a maximum purchase price of $350,000
- Maximum federal rebate: **$6,300**
- The rebate phases out and disappears completely for homes over **$450,000**

**Phase-out formula (for homes between $350,000–$450,000):**
Rebate = $6,300 × [($450,000 − purchase price) ÷ $100,000]

For homes over $450,000, the federal rebate is zero — but provincial rebates may still apply.

## Ontario New Housing Rebate (Most Significant)

Ontario's provincial portion of HST is 8%. Ontario provides its own new housing rebate:
- **36% of the 8% provincial portion**, capped at **$24,000**
- This rebate applies to the **full purchase price with no upper price limit** (unlike the federal rebate)
- A $600,000 new home in Ontario triggers: $600,000 × 8% × 36% = **$17,280 Ontario rebate** (plus zero federal rebate above $450,000)

This is why the Ontario rebate is often the larger piece for expensive new homes.

## Who Can Claim It

**Eligible purchasers:**
- Buyers of new homes from a builder (house or condo)
- Buyers of homes substantially renovated at their own expense
- Owner-builders who construct their own home
- Buyers of new homes purchased for use as a **primary place of residence** (or for a qualifying relative's primary residence)

**Not eligible:**
- Investment properties that will not be used as a primary residence
- Homes resold by the first buyer before occupying them
- Commercial properties

## Builder Assignment vs. Direct Claim

**Builder assignment (most common for new builds):**
Most builders include the HST in the purchase price, then deduct the rebate — so you effectively pay the net amount. The builder processes the rebate claim with the CRA on your behalf. You must sign an assignment form and meet the eligibility conditions.

**If you pay HST separately:**
If HST was charged in addition to the purchase price (less common), you file directly with the CRA using Form GST190 (federal) and Form RC7190-ON (Ontario). The deadline is **two years after the closing date**.

**Substantial renovations:**
If you substantially renovated an existing home (90%+ of the interior gutted), you file directly. Same two-year deadline from when work was substantially complete.

## What Most People Don't Know

- **The rebate applies to rental properties via a different form.** If you buy a new home as a long-term rental, you may qualify for the **GST/HST New Residential Rental Property Rebate** (Form GST524) — different from the primary residence rebate. Many investors miss this.
- **The two-year filing deadline is strict.** If you're claiming directly (not via builder assignment), missing the two-year deadline means losing the rebate permanently.
- **Presale condos purchased before completion have special rules.** If you signed an Agreement of Purchase and Sale before 2023 and the closing is now, the tax calculation uses the rules in effect at the time of the original agreement in some cases.
- **Other provinces have their own provincial rebates.** BC, PEI, Nova Scotia, PEI, and New Brunswick have their own versions — check your province's rules.
- **Substantial renovation threshold is high.** To qualify as a "substantial renovation," 90% of the interior of the existing building must be removed or replaced — this is a high bar and must be documented carefully.
