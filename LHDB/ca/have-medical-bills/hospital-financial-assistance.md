## Overview

Canada's **Medical Expense Tax Credit (METC)** is a non-refundable federal tax credit on eligible medical expenses above a threshold. The credit applies to expenses paid for yourself, your spouse, and your dependent children — and even certain other dependents (parents, grandparents, adult children, and other qualifying relatives) on a separate, more generous calculation.

Despite its scope, the METC is massively underused. Canadians miss it because:
- They don't realize how broad the eligible expense list is
- They don't know they can choose **any 12-month period ending in the tax year** (not just January–December)
- They don't aggregate expenses across family members

## Federal Credit Calculation

You can claim eligible medical expenses that **exceed the lesser of**:
- **3% of your net income**, OR
- **$2,635** (2024 indexed threshold)

The federal credit rate is **15%** of qualifying expenses above the threshold. Provinces add their own credit (typically 5.05%–10%), bringing the combined benefit to roughly **20–25%** of eligible expenses.

**Example**: If you earn $80,000, your 3% threshold is $2,400. If you have $8,000 in eligible expenses, you can claim $5,600 × 15% = **$840 federal credit** + provincial credit.

## Choose Your Best 12-Month Window

A commonly missed planning tip: you can claim medical expenses for **any 12-month period ending in the tax year**, not just the calendar year. If you had a high-cost medical event in October 2023 and another in November 2024, you can claim expenses from November 2023 to October 2024 — capturing both events in a single tax return year.

## Broad List of Eligible Expenses (Partial)

Most Canadians don't realize the following are eligible:

**Dental & Vision**
- Dental work (fillings, crowns, implants, orthodontics)
- Prescription eyeglasses and contact lenses
- Laser eye surgery
- Hearing aids and batteries

**Mental & Physical Health**
- Psychologist and therapist fees (registered professionals)
- Physiotherapy, chiropractic, osteopathy, acupuncture (where provincially regulated)
- Addiction treatment programs (if certified)
- Service animals (including guide dogs and seizure response dogs)

**Assistive Devices & Home Modifications**
- Wheelchairs, crutches, walkers, prosthetics
- Renovations to a home for a person with mobility impairment (ramps, wider doors, walk-in tubs — requires certification by medical practitioner)
- Hospital beds for home use

**Prescription & Non-Prescription**
- Prescription medications
- Cannabis (if prescribed by a licensed practitioner)
- Certain over-the-counter products when prescribed

**Attendant Care**
- Costs for an attendant or nursing home care for a person who qualifies for the Disability Tax Credit
- Full-time attendant care at home

**Other**
- Travel costs to receive medical care (if not available locally) — 40¢/km (2024) or actual travel/accommodation costs if 80+ km from home
- In vitro fertilization procedures
- Surrogacy agency fees and related medical costs

## The Dependent Relative Claim

For eligible medical expenses paid for a **dependent relative** (not your spouse or minor child), the calculation changes:
- The 3%/threshold floor applies to the **dependent's** net income (often very low or zero for elderly parents)
- The credit is refundable up to a maximum under the refundable medical expense supplement for low-income earners

This means if you paid for a parent's dental work or home care, virtually 100% of those expenses (above the parent's low threshold) may be claimable.

## Refundable Supplement for Low-Income Workers

The **Refundable Medical Expense Supplement** is an additional refundable credit for low-income working individuals (income $15,000–$35,000 approximately). Unlike the base METC, this supplement **creates a refund** even if you owe no tax. Maximum $1,567 (2024).

## Tips for Maximizing the METC

1. **Collect all receipts** — Keep every receipt from dentists, pharmacies, therapists, and medical equipment providers throughout the year.
2. **Claim on the lower-income spouse's return** — The threshold (3% of net income or the fixed amount, whichever is lower) is smaller for a lower-income spouse, maximizing the claimable amount.
3. **Coordinate with the DTC** — Many expenses that qualify as METC also support a DTC claim, and DTC approval unlocks attendant care as METC (up to full attendant costs vs. a lower limit without DTC).
4. **Don't forget travel** — Medical travel is frequently missed; keep mileage logs if driving to medical appointments far from home.

## Caveats

- The METC is **non-refundable** for most — it can only reduce taxes owing to zero (except for the refundable supplement above).
- Cosmetic procedures that are elective and not medically necessary are generally **not eligible** (e.g., purely cosmetic surgery, teeth whitening).
- You cannot double-claim: expenses reimbursed by an employer health plan or provincial insurance cannot be claimed as METC.
- Quebec has a separate, more complex medical expense credit — file a provincial return accordingly.
